PART THREE AUTOMATION RULES – CASE MANAGEMENT ASSESSMENT + ASAM CRITERIA

This document explains the automation and prompt logic for Part Three of the behavioral health assessment form.

Part Three includes:

Case Management Assessment severity grid

ASAM Criteria Dimensions 1 through 6

Safety Planning section

ASAM severity logic

Level of care alignment logic that will later feed into Part Four

This section should be generated from the transcript and clinical documentation output, then used to populate the corresponding ReliaTrax form fields.

GENERAL PART THREE RULES

The output must be copy/paste ready.

Use professional clinical language.

Always use the client’s first name.

Write in third person.

Do not use em dashes.

Use full clinical sentences in ASAM narratives.

Use clinical inference when appropriate, but do not fabricate or overstate.

All ratings must match the clinical narrative.

Do not make the client appear more severe or less severe than the transcript supports.

Use the least restrictive clinically appropriate interpretation.

Avoid repetitive language across ASAM dimensions.

Each ASAM dimension should have its own distinct clinical reasoning.

PART A – CASE MANAGEMENT ASSESSMENT GRID

The Case Management Assessment is a checkbox grid with the following severity options:

0 = None
1 = Mild
2 = Moderate
3 = Severe

Categories to score:

Housing

Financial Stressors

Legal

Employment

Education/Vocation

Independent Living

Medical

Social/Natural Supports

Automation logic:

Bastion should determine the severity number for each category.

Zach’s automation should map the severity number to the matching checkbox column.

If Bastion outputs 0, check None.

If Bastion outputs 1, check Mild.

If Bastion outputs 2, check Moderate.

If Bastion outputs 3, check Severe.

Only one checkbox should be selected per category.

CASE MANAGEMENT SCORING GUIDE

Housing

0 None:
Stable housing with no current housing concerns.

1 Mild:
Temporary housing, sober living, transitional housing, or minor housing-related stressors without immediate loss of housing.

2 Moderate:
Unstable housing, risk of losing housing, unsafe housing, significant housing barriers, or housing problems affecting recovery.

3 Severe:
Homelessness, sleeping outside, shelter living, motel living due to lack of stable housing, or immediate inability to meet housing needs.

Financial Stressors

0 None:
Financially stable or no reported financial concerns.

1 Mild:
Mild financial stress that does not significantly impair functioning.

2 Moderate:
Moderate financial stress, limited income, disability application pending, inability to fully meet needs, or financial stress affecting stability.

3 Severe:
No income, inability to meet basic needs, no access to food/shelter/medication, or severe financial instability.

Legal

0 None:
No current legal concerns.

1 Mild:
Minor legal issue, old warrant, ticket, or minimal legal concern without major functional impact.

2 Moderate:
Active legal case, probation/parole, court requirements, CPS involvement, or legal stress affecting functioning.

3 Severe:
Severe legal consequences, incarceration risk, major active charges, legal instability significantly interfering with treatment or safety.

Employment

0 None:
Stable employment or no employment-related concern.

1 Mild:
Mild employment stress or underemployment without major impairment.

2 Moderate:
Unemployed but seeking work, employment instability, disability process, or employment barriers affecting stability.

3 Severe:
Not working, not seeking work, severe employment barriers, inability to work due to significant impairment, or employment issues severely affecting stability.

Education/Vocation

0 None:
No education or vocational concerns.

1 Mild:
Minor educational/vocational concerns or mild barriers.

2 Moderate:
Moderate vocational barriers, lack of training, difficulty maintaining vocational progress, or dropout history affecting functioning.

3 Severe:
Severe educational/vocational impairment or inability to engage in education/vocation due to significant barriers.

Independent Living

0 None:
Independent with activities of daily living and basic self-management.

1 Mild:
Mild difficulty with organization, follow-through, transportation, scheduling, or daily structure.

2 Moderate:
Moderate impairment with independent living skills, needing support with daily functioning, appointments, stability, or life skills.

3 Severe:
Unable to manage independent living needs, severe impairment in daily functioning, or needs intensive support to meet basic needs.

Medical

0 None:
No medical concerns reported.

1 Mild:
Mild medical concerns with minimal impact.

2 Moderate:
Medical conditions requiring care, chronic health concerns, medication needs, pain, surgery, mobility concerns, or moderate functional impact.

3 Severe:
Severe medical needs, unmanaged serious illness, acute medical risk, inability to access care, or medical issues significantly impairing functioning or safety.

Social/Natural Supports

0 None:
Strong support system.

1 Mild:
Some supports available.

2 Moderate:
Limited support, strained supports, isolation, lack of reliable sober supports, or support deficits affecting recovery.

3 Severe:
No meaningful support system, unsafe supports, highly isolated, or social environment significantly increases risk.

PART B – ASAM CRITERIA

ASAM Criteria includes six dimensions:

Dimension 1:
Acute Intoxication and/or Withdrawal Potential

Dimension 2:
Biomedical Conditions and Complications

Dimension 3:
Emotional, Behavioral, Cognitive Conditions or Complications

Dimension 4:
Readiness to Change

Dimension 5:
Relapse, Continued Use or Continued Problem Potential

Dimension 6:
Recovery/Living Environment

ASAM SEVERITY SCALE

0 = None
1 = Mild
2 = Moderate
3 = High
4 = Severe

The severity label must always match the number.

Do not write “moderate” if the score is 1.

Do not write “mild” if the score is 2.

Do not write “high” if the score is 4.

ASAM OUTPUT FORMAT

Each dimension must be formatted exactly like this:

Severity Rating: X (Label)
Narrative paragraph underneath.

Example:

Severity Rating: 2 (Moderate)
David reports a history of opioid use with prior detox episodes and current medication-assisted treatment. He denies current acute withdrawal symptoms, though his relapse history and ongoing stressors create moderate risk for recurrence of use. Functional impact is moderate due to ongoing recovery needs, and continued structured treatment is indicated to support stabilization.

Each ASAM dimension must include:

Severity Rating line

Correct number

Correct label

Narrative explanation underneath

Clinical justification

Functional impact

Risk implications

Support for treatment need or level of care

ASAM NARRATIVE STYLE

Each dimension should usually be 2 to 4 sentences.

The narrative should be detailed enough to justify the severity rating.

The narrative should not be generic.

The narrative should not use identical phrasing across all dimensions.

Each narrative should include the client’s first name.

Preferred language includes:

Functional impact is mild/moderate/high because...
Risk implications are low/moderate/high due to...
Continued structured support is indicated because...
Current symptoms appear to...
Protective factors include...
Barriers include...
Clinical needs include...

Severity guidance:

0 None:
No current withdrawal risk, no recent use, no intoxication, no cravings, no withdrawal symptoms.

1 Mild:

Significant current use, active withdrawal symptoms, high relapse risk, medical or psychiatric complication risk, or possible need for higher monitoring.

4 Severe:
Severe withdrawal risk, dangerous withdrawal history, current intoxication with severe impairment, seizure/DT risk, or clear need for medical detox/withdrawal management.

IMPORTANT SOBER LIVING / LEVEL OF CARE RULE

Sober living is NOT a level of care.

Sober living is a housing arrangement.

Sober living can be recommended as a supportive housing environment, but it should not be written as if it is clinical treatment.

Do not confuse sober living with residential treatment.

Residential levels of care, such as ASAM 3.1 or 3.5, refer to structured clinically managed treatment programs where the client resides on-site and participates in intensive daily programming.

Residential treatment typically includes:

24-hour structured support
daily clinical programming
groups and individual sessions
limited ability to work or engage in outside responsibilities

Do not recommend residential level of care unless client demonstrates:

high clinical instability
inability to function in less restrictive treatment
need for 24-hour therapeutic support
significant ASAM severity
significant safety or stabilization needs

LEVEL OF CARE ALIGNMENT LOGIC

Part Three ASAM ratings should support the later Level of Care recommendation in Part Four.

The Level of Care recommendation must match the overall ASAM severity pattern.

General guidance:

Mostly Mild ratings:
Usually supports Outpatient Level 1.0.

Multiple Moderate ratings:
Consider Intensive Outpatient Program, ASAM Level 2.1.

Any High ratings or significant instability:
Consider higher level of care depending on risk and functioning.

Severe withdrawal risk:
Consider detox/withdrawal management.

Acute psychiatric safety risk:
Consider emergency evaluation, crisis stabilization, or higher psychiatric level of care.

Always select the least restrictive clinically appropriate level of care.

Do not overestimate.

Do not underestimate.

Do not recommend higher levels just because client lives in sober living.

Do not recommend residential treatment unless ASAM severity supports it.

If safety planning is not needed:

Use a clear explanation that references the lack of acute safety concerns and any protective factors.

Example:

David denies suicidal ideation, homicidal ideation, self-harm, violent behavior, and acute withdrawal symptoms. He is currently engaged in treatment, has stable housing in a sober living environment, and reports supportive recovery resources. There are no current risk factors necessitating additional safety planning at this time.

If safety planning is needed:

Use a clear explanation that identifies the risk and why additional planning is clinically indicated.

Example:

Additional safety planning is recommended due to Crystal’s recent suicidal ideation, limited supports, and unstable housing. She would benefit from a written safety plan, crisis resources, and coordination with treatment supports to reduce immediate risk.
